What is Controlled Unclassified Information?

a.       Summary

                                                               i.      CUI is any safeguarded or controlled information created or processed by or on behalf of the executive branch of government or its agencies. All CUI should be protected following NIST 800-171 (CUI Basic), but some agencies, such as the Department of Defense, have additional agency specific regulations on top of the basic provisions (CUI Specified).   (For a list of CUI sources, see The CUI Registry.)

b.       Statutory Definition 32 C.F.R. § 2002.4(h)

                                                               i.      Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) is information the Government creates or possesses, or that an entity creates or possesses for or on behalf of the Government, that a law, regulation, or Government-wide policy requires or permits an agency to handle using safeguarding or dissemination controls.

                                                             ii.      CUI does not include classified or information a non-executive branch entity possesses and maintains in its own systems that did not come from, or was not created or possessed by or for, an executive branch agency or an entity acting for an agency.  Law, regulation, or Government-wide policy may require or permit safeguarding or dissemination controls

1.       Requiring or permitting agencies to control or protect the information but providing no specific controls, which makes the information CUI Basic;

2.       requiring or permitting agencies to control or protect the information and providing specific controls for doing so, which makes the information CUI Specified; or

3.       requiring or permitting agencies to control the information and specifying only some of those controls, which makes the information CUI Specified, but with CUI Basic controls where the authority does not specify.

How do I know if my business area or physical location is covered by CUI regulations? 

·         If it works on a US federal contract or is a supplier on a US federal contract, then it is probably covered. 

·         How do I identify CUI?

                                                               i.      Summary

The government will mark any CUI it gives you with designations from the CUI Marking Handbook.  This can come in the form of physical documents, electronic files, emails, or any other kind of information.  However, you may also generate CUI during your work for the government.  Your contract should clearly define what CUI you produce and how it should be protected.  Make sure this is clearly stated during contract negotiations with the government.

                                                             ii.      CUI Basic

1.       All unclassified information throughout the executive branch that requires any kind of safeguarding or dissemination control is CUI. This includes information provided by the government as well as information generated by the contractor.

2.       Government Provided Information

·         Any CUI provided by the government must be labeled with CUI or agency-specific designations.  32 C.F.R. § 2002.16(3)  (For instance: Unclassified (U), For Official Use Only (U//FOUO), Official Use Only (OUO), Sensitive But Unclassified (SBU).)  For a full list of markings, see the CUI Marking Handbook.

3.       Contractor Generated Information

·         The individual contractor agreement with the government entity should describe what contractor generated data is and is not CUI.   Comments in the Federal Register indicate that the CUI regulation (32 C.F.R. § 2002) was not intended to draw a line defining what Contractor Generated Information is CUI, but rather intended to leave that to contracting practices, and to later additions to the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR).  This area will be handled in an upcoming FAR case 2017-016.   As of May 2019, the case was still in the proposal stage, not yet drafted.  Will be written by the Defense Acquisition Regulatory Council and the Civilian Agency Acquisition Council.

                                                           iii.      CUI Specified

1.       There are many agencies which have agency-specific regulations surrounding CUI.  If an agency’s CUI is CUI Specified, that should be flagged during the contract negotiation with the agency.  A good example of CUI Specified is any work done with the Department of Defense, or DoD:

2.       Defense Projects

a.       Covered Defense Information (CDI) - If the site has information related to a Defense Project, that is probably “Covered Defense Information” (CDI), which is protected both as CUI and as CDI (CUI Specified).  This information includes:

·   Information provided by or on behalf of the DoD that is marked as CUI or identified as CUI in the contract/order.

·   Any information created, used, transmitted, or stored by the contractor in order to complete the contract/order.  DFAR 252.204-7012

b.       Who is responsible for identifying CDI? 

·         Information given to contractors –

The entity generating the document is responsible for identifying any CDI or CUI it provides to the contractor.  It should be clearly marked, using appropriate CUI markings. DoD Manual 5200.01 Vol. 4 (page 9)

·         Information generated by contractors –

The contract between the contractor and the DoD should describe how to identify CDI or CUI generated by the contractor, as well as the necessary controls or protections that should be used to protect it.  5200.01 Vol. 4 (page 10).  MAKE SURE THE CONTRACT CLEARLY SPELLS OUT WHAT IS/IS NOT CUI AND HOW IT MUST BE PROTECTED.

c.       What does a contractor have to do with CDI and CUI on a Defense Project? DFAR 252.204-7012 requirements:

1.       Safeguard covered defense information

·         Implement NIST 800-171 to protect all CUI and CDI

·         Flow requirements down to subcontractors

2.       Report cyber incidents

·         Contractor shall conduct a review for evidence of compromise and rapidly report cyber incidents to DoD at https://dibnet.dod.mil via an incident collection form (ICF).

3.       Submit malicious software

·         Send any malicious software connected with a cyber incident to the DoD Cyber Crime Center (DC3).

4.       Facilitate damage assessment

·         If requested, provide media, information, or your own damage assessment to the Contracting Officer

d.       Note that Defense Projects are subject to additional regulations on top of CUI under the DFAR. Examples below, but other regulations may apply:

·         DFAR 252.239-7010 Cloud Computing Services

·         DFAR 252.204-7012 Safeguarding Covered Defense Information and Cyber Incident Reporting

·         DFAR 252.227-7013 Rights in Technical Data—Noncommercial Items

 

2.       Is the CUI contained and Isolated?

a.       Yes: controls need only be applied to the areas where CUI is contained.

b.       No: controls will need to be applied wherever CUI exists.  CUI will either need to be consolidated, or NIST 800-171 controls will need to be applied to all systems containing CUI, up to the entire IT infrastructure if the CUI is that widespread.

c.       How do I contain CUI?

        There are several different levels of isolation, depending on your needs.

·   Item Level

·         If only a few pieces of information exist in a system or location, securing those items in one place makes the most sense. 

·   Project Level

·         If your business or location deals only with one federal project, creating a physical and logical container for that project (such as a room and a sequestered server or network) within your facility, network, and systems makes the most sense.  All content related to that project can then live in these protected systems. 

·   Location Level

·         If your business or location deals with multiple federal projects, multiple types of CUI, or has multiple employees working on different projects, it may make the most sense to set aside an entire building or floor – and accompanying network, workstations, servers, and periphery systems – for CUI work.  This entire area should meet CUI Basic standards.  Projects working with CUI Specified can then be sequestered at the project level. 

b.      Is the CUI actually controlled?

·   Any system containing CUI must be secure, monitored, and audited.  Risk assessments of Physical location, network, authentication, and infrastructure must be done to ensure CUI only accessible to authorized individuals.

c.       Does the site employ best practices?

·   Even outside of CUI system controls, what are the wider policies at the site?  Do they employ best practices?  (regular backups and security updates, system and network auditing, etc.)
